Local Minima and Non-Convexity

When we train a deep neural network, our ultimate goal is to find the perfect combination of Weights and Biases that results in the lowest possible Loss (Error). You can visualize this as a hiker trying to walk down a foggy mountain range to reach the lowest possible valley. The mountain is the Loss Landscape, and the mathematical algorithm deciding which step to take next is the Optimizer.

In the interactive 3D visualization above, the generated mountain range represents a highly complex, non-convex problem. It has a deep global minimum at $(0,0)$, but it is surrounded by ridges, hills, and most importantly, Local Minima (false valleys). The goal of the optimizer is to start from a high point on the ridge and navigate over the bumps to reach the absolute bottom.

1. Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)

SGD is the "blindfolded hiker" approach. At any given point, it calculates the slope (gradient) directly under its feet and takes a step in the exact opposite direction (downhill). The size of the step is controlled entirely by the Learning Rate ($\eta$).

The "Local Minima" Trap

Select SGD in the visualizer and hit Play. Notice how it slides down the initial slope but quickly gets permanently stuck in a shallow crater high up on the mountain.

Because SGD only looks at the immediate slope, when it reaches the bottom of a small crater, the slope becomes $0$. The algorithm believes it has finished training, completely unaware that a much deeper valley exists just over the next ridge.

SGD Update Rule
$$w = w - \eta \cdot dw$$

Where $dw$ is the current steepness (gradient). If $dw = 0$, movement completely stops.

2. Adding Momentum

How do we escape the traps? We simulate physics. If you roll a heavy boulder down a large hill, it builds up speed (momentum). If it hits a small crater, it has enough energy to roll right back up the other side and escape.

The Rolling Boulder

Select Momentum and hit Play. Instead of just using the current gradient, it maintains a "velocity" ($v$).

Notice how it successfully powers through the local minima that trapped SGD. It accumulates speed on the long descents, allowing it to easily punch through ridges and find the true center valley.

Momentum Update Rule
$$v = \beta \cdot v + (1-\beta) \cdot dw$$ $$w = w - \eta \cdot v$$

Where $\beta \approx 0.9$ controls the "friction" of the landscape.

3. RMSprop (Adaptive Learning Rates)

What if, instead of relying on velocity, we just forced the algorithm to automatically adapt its step size based on the ruggedness of the terrain?

The Adaptive Hiker

Select RMSprop. This optimizer keeps a running average of the squared gradients ($S$).

If a dimension has steep, violent drops, $S$ becomes large, and RMSprop automatically shrinks the step size to avoid blowing up. If a dimension is flat, it boosts the step size. It adapts to the landscape dynamically, avoiding the massive overcorrections SGD suffers from.

RMSprop Update Rule
$$S = \gamma \cdot S + (1-\gamma) \cdot dw^2$$ $$w = w - \frac{\eta}{\sqrt{S} + \epsilon} \cdot dw$$

4. Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation)

Adam is the modern gold standard. It simply combines the best of both worlds: it uses Velocity (Momentum) to easily escape local minima, AND it uses the Squared Gradients (RMSprop) to adapt the learning rate dynamically.

The Ultimate Explorer

Industry Standard

Select All (Compare) in the dropdown and hit Play. You will see exactly why Adam is the default choice for Deep Learning today. Watch the Leaderboard as Adam gracefully combines momentum to punch through the ridges, while using adaptive learning rates to flawlessly spiral down into the exact global minimum.

Adam Complete Update Rule
Momentum (1st Moment) $$v = \beta_1 v + (1-\beta_1) dw$$
RMSprop (2nd Moment) $$S = \beta_2 S + (1-\beta_2) dw^2$$
Final Update (Simplified) $$w = w - \frac{\eta}{\sqrt{S} + \epsilon} \cdot v$$
